### 1. Dominant Frameworks & Shifts
The landscape continues to be defined by three primary architectural patterns, with a noticeable shift towards **lightweight, specialized runtimes** over monolithic orchestrators.

*   **CrewAI & LangGraph** remain the most starred repositories for high-level, declarative multi-agent orchestration. However, community discussion indicates increased focus on **deployment cost and latency** as limiting factors for production use.
*   **AutoGen** (Microsoft) maintains strong institutional backing but shows slower public commit velocity compared to 2025. The conversation has pivoted to its use as a **research benchmark** rather than a primary deployment framework.
*   **Emerging Hotspot:** **Cline** (by cline-cli) and **Aider** are gaining traction in the "coding agent" niche. Their trend is driven by a tight integration with developer workflows (direct git operations, in-editor usage) rather than abstract agent design.

### 2. The "Runtime" Trend
A significant trend is the decoupling of agent logic from execution environments, mirroring fleet interests in `cudaclaw` and `flux-runtime`.

*   **LangChain's LangGraph** is increasingly marketed as a **runtime-agnostic state machine compiler**. Their recent blog posts discuss compilation targets for WebAssembly and dedicated AI chips.
*   **New Entrants:** Projects like **`agent-runtime`** (Rust-based) and **`inference-runtime`** are appearing, focusing solely on low-latency execution, tool calling, and context management, explicitly leaving "orchestration logic" to the user.
*   **Observation:** The market is separating into **orchestration layers** (CrewAI, LangGraph Studio) and **execution runtimes** (agent-runtime, potentially `cudaclaw`). This creates a potential channel (P1) for fleet technology to integrate as a high-performance runtime backend.

### 3. Research & Academic Frontier
Academic pre-prints (arXiv) from Q1 2026 frequently reference agent frameworks for **reproducibility**, but highlight gaps.

*   **Key Gap Identified:** Most frameworks provide poor support for **long-running, stateful agent simulations** — the exact domain of `fleet-simulator` and `holodeck-rust`. Papers building simulated environments often resort to custom Flask servers or direct OpenAI calls, not leveraging existing agent SDKs.
*   **"Agent Benchmarks"** are a hot topic. New benchmarks like **AgentBoard** and **SWE-Agent** are pushing frameworks to demonstrate performance on concrete tasks (e.g., fixing GitHub issues, completing complex web tasks), moving beyond simple Q&A demos.

### 4. Fleet Adjacency Analysis
*   **P0 (Negative Space):** The fleet should likely avoid building another general-purpose, high-level orchestrator in Python. That channel is crowded and optimization (P2) there would be difficult.
*   **P1 (Safe Channels):**
    1.  **Specialized Simulation Runtime:** The gap in simulation support is a clear channel. `holodeck-rust` (sentiment-aware NPCs) and `fleet-simulator` could be positioned as frameworks for **research-grade multi-agent simulation**.
    2.  **GPU-Native Execution:** The trend towards specialized runtimes presents a channel for `cudaclaw` if it can demonstrate order-of-magnitude latency improvements for tool-calling or LLM inference within an agent loop.
    3.  **Constraint-Based Planning:** `constraint-theory-core` (geometric snapping) occupies a unique niche. If framed as a "planning and constraint satisfaction module for embodied agents," it could integrate with robotics/VR agent frameworks.
