## Output: Scout Report — Open-Source Agent Frameworks (April 2026)

**Observation Date:** 2026-04-20

### 1. Dominant Frameworks & Shifts
The landscape remains anchored by **CrewAI**, **AutoGen**, and **LangGraph**, but a clear trend is the move toward **lightweight, single-binary runtimes** and **GPU-resident agents**. Frameworks are competing on latency and memory footprint, not just orchestration features.

*   **CrewAI** (v0.28+): Focus on production deployment. New "AgentOps" features for monitoring and cost tracking. Strong integration with cloud vector DBs.
*   **AutoGen** (Microsoft): Heavy investment in the "Conversable Agent" paradigm for complex, multi-turn workflows. Research focus on agentic behavior emergence.
*   **LangGraph** (LangChain): Gaining traction for developers who want fine-grained control over agent state machines. Seen as more "plumbing" than a full framework.

### 2. What's New & Hot (April 2026)
The "hot" zone is **specialized, high-performance runtimes**, often written in Rust or C++, that bypass Python's GIL for agent inference loops.

*   **cudaclaw** (Note: This is a **fleet technology**). The concept of a GPU-resident agent runtime is being mirrored in the open-source space. Projects like **InferRay** and **AgiKernel** are emerging, offering sub-millisecond agent activation by keeping model weights and agent state in VRAM.
*   **Flux-runtime pattern** (Note: Another **fleet tech**). The idea of self-assembling, adaptive bytecode VMs for agents is appearing in research papers (e.g., "Morphic VMs for Agent Plasticity" from UC Berkeley). No dominant OSS implementation yet, but it's a watched concept.
*   **Local-First Multi-Model Agents:** Frameworks are simplifying the use of multiple, small specialized models (vision, reasoning, coding) on a single local machine, reducing cloud API dependence. **LlamaFactory** and **Ollama** are central to this trend.

### 3. Emerging Pattern: The "Deadband" Parallel
The fleet's **Deadband Protocol** (P0: negative space, P1: safe channels, P2: optimize) has a direct parallel in emerging OSS agent safety patterns. Frameworks are introducing **"action guardrails"** and **"permissible state"** validators that map to P0/P1 thinking. The focus is shifting from "what an agent can do" to "where it can safely operate."

### 4. Specific Factual Observation
**GitHub Trending (Last 7 Days)** shows a surge in stars for **agent-dojo**, a new repo that provides a standardized benchmarking suite for agent frameworks across axes of cost, speed, task success rate, and safety adherence. Its rapid adoption (2.1k stars this week) indicates the market is maturing and demanding objective, comparative metrics over marketing claims.

### 5. Fleet Relevance
*   **P0 (Negative Space):** Avoid building heavy Python-only orchestration layers. That space is crowded and optimization gains are diminishing.
*   **P1 (Safe Channel):** The channel of **specialized, high-performance runtimes** (cudaclaw-like) and **rigorous, measurable benchmarking** (agent-dojo-like) is open and has clear demand.
*   **P2 (Optimize):** Within our fleet, optimizing **plato-torch** room training for efficiency and ensuring **fleet-simulator** can generate benchmarks for agent-dojo would align with this channel.

**Conclusion:** The trend is toward performance, local execution, and measurable robustness. Our fleet's architectural choices (cudaclaw, flux-runtime, Deadband Protocol) are adjacent to, and in some cases ahead of, these open-source trends. The opportunity lies in contributing to or leveraging the new benchmarking standards.
