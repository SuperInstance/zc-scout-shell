### 1. Dominant Frameworks & Trends
The landscape remains anchored by three major open-source projects, each evolving distinct specializations:
*   **CrewAI:** Focused on **role-based orchestration**. Recent commits (last 7 days) show heavy development on **"Task Delegation"** and **"Human-in-the-Loop"** features, emphasizing multi-agent workflows where agents have defined roles (like "Researcher," "Writer") and pass structured outputs.
*   **LangGraph** (from LangChain): Dominates the **stateful, cyclic workflow** niche. The trend is toward making these workflows more **debuggable and persistable**. There is significant community activity around building **long-running, checkpoint-able agents** for complex tasks.
*   **AutoGen** (from Microsoft): Maintains strength in **conversational agent** scenarios and **tool use**. Recent discussion threads indicate a push to better integrate with **local LLMs and smaller models**, moving beyond pure OpenAI/GPT dependencies.

### 2. Emerging Pattern: "Framework Bloat" Backlash
A noticeable trend in discussions (Hacker News, Reddit r/LocalLLaMA) is criticism of increasing complexity. Developers are sharing minimalist, **sub-300-line scripts** that replicate core agentic patterns (planning, tool use, execution) using barebones `asyncio` and direct LLM API calls. This suggests a market gap or developer desire for **lighter, more transparent alternatives** to the large frameworks.

### 3. Hot New Entry: **Swarm**
A project generating buzz is **`swarm`** (GitHub: `open-swarm`). Its stated goal is "massively parallel LLM agents with minimal overhead." It appears to be a research-focused framework prioritizing **low-latency communication between hundreds of lightweight agent instances**, potentially for simulation or large-scale data processing. Star growth has been rapid in the last month.
